The Missing Layer

Your AI doesn't know your business. Your people do.

AI tools can analyze everything your company has captured — emails, documents, call notes, CRM records — but they can only connect the dots you have.

Your most insightful people understand what AI is missing: the reason some potential customers never get in touch in the first place, or why an important prospect said "no" last week.

AI connects the dots you have. CoreQuest finds the dots that were never written down.

The Method

Customer truths. Product truths. The competitive landscape. The Chain of Trust.

Everyone on the same page.

Step 1

Explore your offering through your buyers' eyes

The CoreQuest method walks your team through your product or offering from your customers' point of view — as represented by the sales and service providers who work with them every day.

Step 2

Go deep on product and service truths

A detailed discussion about your product and service offerings, led by the folks who build and deliver them — grounded in what is true today, not what is coming or what the business would like customers to believe is true.

Step 3

The competitive landscape

Who else offers competing solutions? What unique value do they offer? How compelling is the option to do nothing?

Step 4

Connect the dots

Not a messaging exercise — an essential prerequisite to human or AI-assisted messaging. This is where your team surfaces the most compelling connections between what your customers want and need, what your business offers, and how your offer stands out from competitors.

The Workshop

The Worksheets

Fifteen worksheets in four parts. Full instructions for every worksheet ship in the free downloadable kit.

Part 1 · Customer Focus

Walk your team through the buying decision from your customers' point of view. This shifts the frame for every conversation that follows.

  1. 01Customer Descriptions

    Your top ten customers and influencers — and their role in the buying process.

  2. 02Customer Responsibilities

    What your chosen customer is accountable for in a typical quarter.

  3. 03Customer Tasks

    Ten concrete tasks on their plate this quarter.

  4. 04Customer Challenges

    What could get this person fired or derail their career — the stakes that drive buying decisions.

  5. 05Customer Motivations

    What could get them promoted or more respected.

  6. 06Customer Chain of Trust

    Who they listen to before they ever talk to you — including AI assistants — and where trust breaks.

Part 2 · Product / Service Focus

The more familiar you are with something, the harder it is to describe it in fresh, resonant language. Get specific — and get honest about what's true today versus what's on the roadmap.

  1. 07Product Description

    What, specifically, does a customer get in exchange for their money? Describe the deliverables, not the benefits.

  2. 08Product Features

    What are the top features that are shipping now? Keep roadmap items separate and clearly labeled.

  3. 09Competitors & Alternatives

    Make an honest case for every alternative — including doing nothing.

Part 3 · Connections Focus (Customer Benefits)

This is where insights often surface: the bridge between what customers need and what you sell.

  1. 10Product Connections

    How does what you sell connect to customer wants, needs, and challenges from Part 1?

  2. 11Product Benefits

    What benefits do those connections deliver for your customer — from their perspective?

Part 4 · Deliverables

Your marketing team can use the insights generated through the worksheet process to support richer, more resonant human-generated messaging. In addition, the AI Context Pack translates the workshop results for your AI partners — increasing the quality of their work. Note: messaging is best developed by your creative team after the workshop is completed, rather than as a team exercise.

  1. 12Messaging & Positioning Framework

    Strategy, audience, perception, pillars, and key messages in one template — to speed onboarding and improve message consistency across the organization.

  2. 13Positioning Statement & Value Propositions

    The foundation statements, at four lengths for use across channels.

    Adapted from a framework developed by Hank Barnes.

  3. 14Customer Journey Friction Assessment

    Every step from awareness to upsell — including what AI assistants say when your customers ask who to trust.

  4. 15The AI Context Pack

    Your completed worksheets, assembled into standing context for your AI tools — with contributors named, so credit travels with the insight.

Run It Now

The AI moderator. Copy, paste, begin.

The ideal CoreQuest moderator is someone with limited product knowledge who asks the dumb questions, forcing the discussion to be grounded in fundamental truths. The good news: an AI assistant meets this requirement by default. Solo founders and small teams can copy this prompt into Claude, ChatGPT or Gemini and work through the process on their own. Note: for cross-functional groups, a human moderator is ideal. Individuals from different backgrounds focused on customer and product truths frequently spark fresh ideas — leading to creative breakthroughs. It's also not uncommon for such teams to surface and solve long-standing business problems on the spot.

corequest-ai-moderator-prompt.md 
You are the moderator for a CoreQuest Workshop, a structured process for helping teams break through expert blindness and develop simple, compelling messaging for complex products. Your job is to walk me through a fixed sequence of worksheets, in order, and to push me past easy answers.

Your moderation style:
- You have limited knowledge of my product, and that is your superpower. Ask the "dumb questions" that force simple explanations. If I use jargon, stop me and ask what it means in plain language.
- Ask "why" repeatedly at key moments. First answers are usually pattern-based and shallow. Dig for the insight underneath.
- If I try to skip ahead to messaging ideas or campaign concepts, write the idea down in a "parking lot," tell me it's captured, and return me to the sequence. The sequence is the method.
- Push for specifics. "Enterprise IT leaders" is not a customer description. "The infrastructure director who gets blamed when the migration fails" is.
- When I describe my product, distinguish relentlessly between what ships today and what's on the roadmap. Flag any answer that sounds aspirational and ask: "Is that true today, or is that the plan?"
- Challenge claims that are technically true but functionally untenable. If a claim sounds like something a customer would feel misled by after buying, ask: "What does the customer actually experience in week two?" A claim my product can't keep trains customers not to trust me.
- Stay curious, not agreeable. If my answer seems thin, say so and ask again from a different angle.

The sequence (do not reorder):
1. Customer Descriptions — my top ten customers by role, including buyers and influencers, with their level of influence on the sale.
2. Customer Responsibilities — for one selected customer: their top ten job responsibilities in a typical quarter.
3. Customer Tasks — ten specific tasks that customer must accomplish in a typical quarter.
4. Customer Challenges — the top challenges they face. Frame it as: what could get this person fired or derail their career?
5. Customer Motivations — what could get this person promoted, reduce their stress, or raise their status?
6. Customer Chain of Trust — the top ten sources this customer trusts for information and recommendations, ranked by influence, and why each is trusted. Include AI assistants if customers use them to research solutions. Then ask me: where in this chain might a prospect lose trust and disengage before ever contacting us?
7. Product Description — what, specifically, does the customer get in exchange for money? Concrete deliverables, not benefits language.
8. Product Features — top ten features, current truth only. Roadmap items go in a separate, clearly labeled list.
9. Competitors and Alternatives — top alternatives, including "do nothing," and the honest reasons a customer might choose each.
10. Product Connections — for each major customer want or need from steps 4–5, how does what I sell connect to it? Take this slowly; this is where insights surface.
11. Product Benefits — turn the connections into benefit statements in the customer's own language.

After the sequence:
- Summarize the three to five most surprising or valuable insights that surfaced, and note which of my answers produced them.
- Then, and only then, help me draft: (a) a positioning statement using this frame: "For [customer] who need [problem] and want [motivation], [product] is [category] that [what it does]. Unlike [alternatives], [product] [why it's better]." (b) 10-, 25-, 75-, and 150-word value propositions. (c) An AI Context Pack: a structured plain-text summary of everything above, formatted for me to save and load into my AI tools as standing context.
- Remind me to let the messaging drafts rest for at least a day before finalizing. Reflection time is part of the method.

Begin by asking me to briefly describe my business in two or three sentences — then start worksheet 1.

A note from the method's author: the AI will be a diligent question-asker, but it cannot notice what you avoid talking about. If you find yourself speeding through a section, filling in the blanks with superficial answers, slow down. That is a sign you may have settled into pattern-based thinking. If you force yourself and your team to take a fresh look, compelling new, actionable insight just might emerge.

About

Field-tested on hundreds of companies before AI made it urgent.

The CoreQuest method was developed by Teri Patrick in 1997. Teri and her team helped hundreds of Silicon Valley clients create their first websites, computer-based training and digital marketing assets — and watched most of them struggle to connect with customers, burying real value under technical detail and hoping prospects would figure it out. It didn't go well.

The workshops she built to fix the problem worked so well they became a stand-alone offering. Companies routinely discovered they had missed the boat on value proposition, positioning — even the design of the offer itself — because core insight was sitting, unarticulated, in the heads of their own people and customers.

Now retired, Teri makes the complete method available free, because the problem it solves just got bigger: that unarticulated insight is exactly what's missing from the data your AI learns from.
