The Missing Layer

Your AI doesn't know your business. Your people do.

AI tools can analyze everything your company has captured — emails, documents, call notes, CRM records — but they can only connect the dots you have.

Your most insightful people understand what AI is missing: the reason some potential customers never get in touch in the first place, or why an important prospect said "no" last week.

AI connects the dots you have. CoreQuest finds the dots that were never written down.

From SEO to AI Visibility

Clear, focused writing matters — even if your primary audience is AI.

Your customers are asking AI to recommend products and services. AI is reading your content and reviews for relevance and trustworthiness — and recommending whichever companies it can clearly understand. Search engine optimization got you ranked. AI visibility gets you recommended, and it rewards clear, insight-rich writing over keyword volume.

The Method

Customer truths. Product truths. The competitive landscape. The Chain of Trust.

Everyone on the same page.

Step 1

Explore your offering through your buyers' eyes

The CoreQuest method walks your team through your product or offering from your customers' point of view — as represented by the sales and service providers who work with them every day.

Step 2

Go deep on product and service truths

A detailed discussion about your product and service offerings, led by the folks who build and deliver them — grounded in what is true today, not what is coming or what the business would like customers to believe is true.

Step 3

The competitive landscape

Who else offers competing solutions? What unique value do they offer? How compelling is the option to do nothing?

Step 4

Connect the dots

Not a messaging exercise — an essential prerequisite to human or AI-assisted messaging. This is where your team surfaces the most compelling connections between what your customers want and need, what your business offers, and how your offer stands out from competitors.

The Workshop

The complete program, right here.

Everything below is the full CoreQuest method — no email wall, no teaser version. Expand any worksheet to read it, or download the whole kit as Markdown to print, adapt, or drop straight into your AI tools. Work the sheets in order; the sequence is the method.

Part 1 · Customer Focus

Focus your team's attention on your customer before anything else. Even when the information isn't new to everyone, time spent in the details of your customers' world shifts the team's frame of reference for every conversation that follows. New insights come from looking at familiar information in new ways.

01Customer Descriptions

List and describe your top ten customers. Customers include individuals empowered to make buying decisions as well as individuals who strongly influence sales by recommending or advocating for your product with the ultimate buyer. Define each person's role and level of influence.

02Customer Responsibilities

Select a specific customer or related set of customers from your list. What are the top ten job responsibilities they need to fulfill in any given month or quarter?

03Customer Tasks

For that customer, what are ten job tasks they need to accomplish in a typical quarter?

04Customer Challenges

What are the top challenges they need to overcome in any given month or quarter? Run this exercise as: top things that could get your customer fired or derail their career. The point is to get everyone focused on specific problems that motivate action.

Why this mattersThese emotional and career stakes drive buying decisions but almost never appear in any document an AI could learn from. This worksheet is where they finally get written down.
05Customer Motivations

What are ten top personal motivations to consider? Run it as: what are the top ten things that could get this person promoted, reduce their job stress, or help them gain status — whatever fits your customer base.

06Customer Chain of Trust

Who do your customers trust? Describe the top ten trusted sources of information and recommendations related to your offering, ranked by influence — and why each is trusted. Especially useful for marketing strategy decisions.

Why this mattersAI assistants now sit on nearly every customer's chain of trust — often above analysts and review sites. Include them. Then ask the question no dataset can answer: where in this chain might a prospect lose trust and disengage before ever contacting us?

Part 2 · Product / Service Focus

Finally — your product. Why spend time describing what everyone in the room already knows? Because the more familiar you are with something, the harder it is to find fresh ways of describing it. And beware clever, benefits-only marketing: if buyers can't tell what, specifically, you're offering in exchange for their money, email or attention, no amount of inspiration saves you. One more warning, learned the hard way: don't train customers not to trust you. A claim can be technically true but functionally untenable — glamorous in the deck, disappointing in week two. Organizations incentivize exactly this when they push product marketing to hit targets fast. The current-truth discipline below is the antidote.

07Product Description

What is your product or service — that is, what will a customer specifically get in exchange for money (or time, or attention)? List ten concrete things. Deliverables, not benefits language.

08Product Features

The top ten features that benefit customers. Product experts must be straightforward here about current product truth versus roadmap — no "marketing ahead of capabilities." Your sales and marketing colleagues are better partners when they know what's real versus aspirational.

Why this mattersAn AI grounded on a document that blurs shipped and roadmap will confidently market vaporware. It has no instinct for the difference. Keep two clearly labeled lists, and date them.
09Competitors & Alternatives

The top alternatives to your product or service — one of which may be "doing nothing." If so, capture the honest reasons a customer would do nothing rather than seek a solution.

Part 3 · Connections Focus

The groundwork for messaging and strategy: how what you sell connects to what customers want and need. Ideas for campaigns will surface here — capture them in a parking lot and keep going. Allow at least a day of reflection before follow-up discussion; the delay is part of the method.

10Product Connections

For each major customer want, need, challenge or motivation from Part 1: how does what you sell connect to it? Take this slowly. This is where the insights surface.

11Product Benefits

Turn the connections into benefit statements — in the customer's own language — ready for use in marketing materials.

Part 4 · Deliverables

The worksheets above become two deliverables built from the same insight: messaging and positioning for your human channels, and the AI Context Pack that grounds your AI tools. Create both. Messaging is not completed in the group session — give your creatives reflection time with access to subject-matter experts. Note: it typically takes twelve to eighteen months of consistent effort to land a message in a market — refine wording continuously, but don't churn core messaging.

12Messaging & Positioning Framework

A structured template covering strategy, audience definition, current and future perception, customer challenges, tagline, value propositions at 10–25, 75 and 150 words, messaging pillars and aligned key messages. Full template in the downloadable kit.

13Positioning Statement & Value Propositions

The foundation statements: For [customer] who need [problem] and want [motivation], [product] is [category] that [what it does]. Unlike [alternatives], [product] [why it's better]. Plus value propositions at four lengths for use across channels. Adapted from a framework developed by Hank Barnes.

14Customer Journey Friction Assessment

Walk the full journey — awareness, exploring solutions, evaluating alternatives, justification and approval, acquisition, implementation, expansion, support, upgrades, upselling — and capture the friction at every step. Is psychological safety a factor? How painful is budget approval and vendor onboarding? What are the switching costs?

Test it"Exploring solutions" means AI assistants as well as search. Ask ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini your customers' problem-solving questions — verbatim, in their words. Does the AI mention you? Accurately? Currently? Who does it recommend instead, and what have they written down that you haven't? Watching an AI recommend a competitor is often the moment skeptics lean in.
15The AI Context Pack

The deliverable that grounds your AI: your completed worksheets, assembled into a set of plain-text documents formatted for the AI tools your team uses — a Claude Project, a custom GPT, a sales-enablement tool. Nine sections: who you serve, what keeps them up at night, who they trust, current product truth, real alternatives, why it matters, how to sound like you (and them), where the journey breaks — and contributors and provenance, so credit travels with the insight. Once deployed, every email, page and campaign your AI drafts is grounded in your workshop instead of generic filler.

Run It Now

The AI moderator. Copy, paste, begin.

The ideal CoreQuest moderator was always someone with limited product knowledge who asks the dumb questions — which describes an AI assistant meeting your business for the first time, exactly. For solo founders and small teams, copy this prompt into Claude, ChatGPT or Gemini and run the full sequence today. For cross-functional groups, a human moderator is still the real thing: reading the scowling skeptic in the back of the room is human work.

corequest-ai-moderator-prompt.md 
You are the moderator for a CoreQuest Workshop, a structured process for helping teams break through expert blindness and develop simple, compelling messaging for complex products. Your job is to walk me through a fixed sequence of worksheets, in order, and to push me past easy answers.

Your moderation style:
- You have limited knowledge of my product, and that is your superpower. Ask the "dumb questions" that force simple explanations. If I use jargon, stop me and ask what it means in plain language.
- Ask "why" repeatedly at key moments. First answers are usually pattern-based and shallow. Dig for the insight underneath.
- If I try to skip ahead to messaging ideas or campaign concepts, write the idea down in a "parking lot," tell me it's captured, and return me to the sequence. The sequence is the method.
- Push for specifics. "Enterprise IT leaders" is not a customer description. "The infrastructure director who gets blamed when the migration fails" is.
- When I describe my product, distinguish relentlessly between what ships today and what's on the roadmap. Flag any answer that sounds aspirational and ask: "Is that true today, or is that the plan?"
- Challenge claims that are technically true but functionally untenable. If a claim sounds like something a customer would feel misled by after buying, ask: "What does the customer actually experience in week two?" A claim my product can't keep trains customers not to trust me.
- Stay curious, not agreeable. If my answer seems thin, say so and ask again from a different angle.

The sequence (do not reorder):
1. Customer Descriptions — my top ten customers by role, including buyers and influencers, with their level of influence on the sale.
2. Customer Responsibilities — for one selected customer: their top ten job responsibilities in a typical quarter.
3. Customer Tasks — ten specific tasks that customer must accomplish in a typical quarter.
4. Customer Challenges — the top challenges they face. Frame it as: what could get this person fired or derail their career?
5. Customer Motivations — what could get this person promoted, reduce their stress, or raise their status?
6. Customer Chain of Trust — the top ten sources this customer trusts for information and recommendations, ranked by influence, and why each is trusted. Include AI assistants if customers use them to research solutions. Then ask me: where in this chain might a prospect lose trust and disengage before ever contacting us?
7. Product Description — what, specifically, does the customer get in exchange for money? Concrete deliverables, not benefits language.
8. Product Features — top ten features, current truth only. Roadmap items go in a separate, clearly labeled list.
9. Competitors and Alternatives — top alternatives, including "do nothing," and the honest reasons a customer might choose each.
10. Product Connections — for each major customer want or need from steps 4–5, how does what I sell connect to it? Take this slowly; this is where insights surface.
11. Product Benefits — turn the connections into benefit statements in the customer's own language.

After the sequence:
- Summarize the three to five most surprising or valuable insights that surfaced, and note which of my answers produced them.
- Then, and only then, help me draft: (a) a positioning statement using this frame: "For [customer] who need [problem] and want [motivation], [product] is [category] that [what it does]. Unlike [alternatives], [product] [why it's better]." (b) 10-, 25-, 75-, and 150-word value propositions. (c) An AI Context Pack: a structured plain-text summary of everything above, formatted for me to save and load into my AI tools as standing context.
- Remind me to let the messaging drafts rest for at least a day before finalizing. Reflection time is part of the method.

Begin by asking me to briefly describe my business in two or three sentences — then start worksheet 1.

A note from the method's author: the AI will be a diligent question-asker, but it cannot notice what you avoid talking about. If you find yourself speeding through a section, filling in the blanks with superficial answers, slow down there. That discomfort is often where new, actionable insight is ready to emerge.

About

Field-tested on hundreds of companies before AI made it urgent.

The CoreQuest method was developed by Teri Patrick in 1997. Teri and her team helped hundreds of Silicon Valley clients create their first websites, computer-based training and digital marketing assets — and watched most of them struggle to connect with customers, burying real value under technical detail and hoping prospects would figure it out. It didn't go well.

The workshops she built to fix it worked so well they became a stand-alone offering. Companies routinely discovered they had missed the boat on value proposition, positioning — even the design of the offer itself — because core insight was sitting, unarticulated, in the heads of their own people and customers.

Now retired, Teri makes the complete method available free, because the problem it solves just got bigger: that unarticulated insight is exactly what's missing from the data your AI learns from.
